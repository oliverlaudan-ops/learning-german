German Alphabet

The German alphabet has 26 letters like English, plus 4 special characters: ä, ö, ü, and ß.

Vowels & Umlauts

German vowels can be short or long, and the umlauts (ä, ö, ü) change the sound significantly.

Umlaut Pronunciation Guide

  • ä - Like the "e" in "bed" (Mädchen = girl)
  • ö - Round your lips for "o" but say "e" (schön = beautiful)
  • ü - Round your lips for "u" but say "i" (über = over)

Short vs. Long Vowels

Vowel length changes meaning in German:

  • Stadt (city) - short a vs. Staat (state) - long a
  • Mitte (middle) - short i vs. Miete (rent) - long i
  • offen (open) - short o vs. Ofen (oven) - long o

Consonants

Some German consonants differ from English. Pay attention to these key differences.

Key Consonant Differences

  • w - Pronounced like English "v" (was = vas)
  • v - Often pronounced like English "f" (Vater = fater)
  • z - Always pronounced "ts" (Zeit = tsait)
  • ß - Sharp S, like "ss" in "hiss" (Straße = shtrah-seh)
  • sp/st - At start of syllable, pronounced "shp/sht" (Sport = shport)

CH Sounds

German has two distinct CH sounds that don't exist in English.

The Two CH Sounds

Ich-Laut (soft CH)

After front vowels (e, i, ä, ö, ü) and consonants. Similar to "h" in "hue" but softer.

Examples: ich, mich, richtig, durch

Ach-Laut (hard CH)

After back vowels (a, o, u). Like clearing your throat gently.

Examples: ach, Buch, doch, machen

The R Sound

German R is very different from English R. It's pronounced in the back of the throat.

Pronunciation Tips

  • German R is a guttural sound, made in the back of the throat
  • At the end of syllables, it often becomes a vowel sound (er → ah)
  • Practice: gargle water, then try to make that sound without water
  • Don't roll your R like in Spanish or Italian

Vowel Combinations

Certain vowel combinations have consistent pronunciations in German.

Common Combinations

ei/ai - like "eye" (mein, Mai)
ie/ei - long "ee" (Liebe, sie)
eu/äu - like "oy" (Freude, Häuser)
au - like "ow" (Haus, Maus)
sch - "sh" sound (Schule, Fisch)
st/sp - "sht/shp" at start (Stadt, Sport)

Word Stress

Correct stress is crucial for being understood in German.

Stress Patterns

  • Most German words are stressed on the first syllable
  • Words with prefixes: stress often on the prefix (Únterricht, Ánfang)
  • Foreign words often keep their original stress (Restauránt, Proféssor)
  • Compound words: stress the first part (Áutobahn, Káffeesatz)

Tongue Twisters

Fun way to practice difficult sounds and improve fluency!

Classic German Tongue Twisters

Fischers Fritze fischt frische Fische.
Fritze Fischer fishes fresh fish.

Brautkleid bleibt Brautkleid und Blaukraut bleibt Blaukraut.
Wedding dress stays wedding dress and red cabbage stays red cabbage.

Wenn Fliegen hinter Fliegen fliegen, fliegen Fliegen Fliegen nach.
When flies fly behind flies, flies fly after flies.
